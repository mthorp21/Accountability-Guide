The Oz Principle
"A personal choice to rise above one's circumstances and demonstrate the ownership necessary for achieving desired results."
1

See it

Obtain others' perspectives. Communicate openly. Ask for and offer feedback. Hear the hard things to see reality clearly.

2

Own it

Be personally invested. Learn from successes and failures. Align work with desired results. Act on the feedback you receive.

3

Solve it

Ask "What else can I do?" Overcome boundaries. Deal creatively with obstacles. Take the necessary risks.

4

Do it

Do what you say you'll do. Focus on top priorities. Stay above the line. Sustain an environment of trust.

Coach back to center

Every time the standard slips, your job is to coach the Gardener back to center. Not tomorrow. Not after the rush. Now. Coaching is correction with dignity.

Constant gentle pressure
Constant
Be specific. Bring it up and correct every time. Inconsistency validates the behavior.
Gentle
Never correct in a way that denies someone their dignity. Coaching is correction with dignity.
Pressure
Standards are standards. Bring the action back to center. No exceptions for busy shifts.

Communication is key

Communication has as much to do with context as content. Three questions before every coaching moment:

Who

When
If the moment passes without correction, you have validated the behavior.
Why
The team member must understand why the standard exists, not just that they missed it.

What's happening

This Gardener shows up, performs, helps others, and raises the floor of your shift. They don't need correction — they need investment. If you are only managing the problems on your team, you are ignoring the people who are carrying it.

How to open the conversation
Manager says:

"I want to be direct with you — you are one of the best Gardeners on this team and I don't say that enough. The way you handled [specific situation] last week is exactly what we are trying to build here. I want to make sure this place is working for you the same way you are working for it. What do you need from me?"

What to listen for
  • Are they being recognized by their peers, not just you?
  • Do they have a clear path to grow — more shifts, a trainer role, a leadership track?
  • Are they carrying extra weight because others aren't pulling their share?
  • Is anything in their schedule or environment starting to grind them down?
How to close
Manager says:

"I am going to give you more responsibility because you have earned it. That is not extra work for the same pay — we are going to talk about what that looks like. I need you here and I want to make sure you know that."

Remember: Praise more than you criticize. If you are not praising someone more than you correct them, one of two things is true: they are doing too many things wrong, or you have let them stay on the team longer than you should have. Either way, an adjustment is required.
Can + won't
Skill without will — Coach the why
Has the ability. Chooses not to use it. A direct contradiction of "Run It Like You Own It."
＋
What's happening

This is the most frustrating quadrant because the skill is there. The Gardener knows how to do the job. They are choosing not to. This is an attitude or motivation problem, and it requires you to coach the why before you coach the behavior. Correcting actions without addressing beliefs will not hold.

How to open the conversation
Manager says:

"I want to have an honest conversation with you because I think you are capable of more than what I am seeing right now. When [specific situation], you have the skills to handle that the right way — I have seen you do it. So I am not here to question your ability. I want to understand what is going on."

Questions that uncover the why
  • "Is there something going on outside of work that is affecting how you show up here?"
  • "Is there something about this job or this team that is not working for you right now?"
  • "Do you feel like your effort here is being recognized? If not, tell me more."
  • "What would need to change for you to feel good about coming to work?"
How to close — with pressure
Manager says:

"I hear you and I appreciate you being honest. Here is what I need from you going forward: [specific behavior]. I am going to check in with you on this every shift. Not because I am watching you — because I am invested in you. But I also need you to know that the standard does not change. If we cannot move this together, we are going to need to have a different conversation."

Document everything. Date, what was said, what the expectation is, and what the follow-up looks like. If this conversation happens more than twice without movement, you are in the bottom 20% decision zone, not the coaching zone.
Can't + will
Will without skill — Train and develop
Wants to succeed. Lacks the capability. This is a training problem, not a character problem.
＋
What's happening

This Gardener has attitude and effort. The gap is skill. Before you coach, verify they actually know the standard. When actions are below standard, there are three reasons: they don't know the standard, they don't care about it, or they have low personal standards. This quadrant is the first case. That is on the system, not the Gardener.

How to open the conversation
Manager says:

"I want to check in on how things are going because I can see you are working hard. I also want to make sure we are setting you up with everything you need to succeed here. Can you walk me through how you handle [specific task]? I want to see where you are at so I can help you get better."

What to do after the conversation
  • Assign a trainer from your top 20% to work alongside them — not to babysit, to mentor.
  • Give a specific, time-bound improvement target: "By next Friday, I want to see [behavior] on every table."
  • Check in daily. Short check-ins, not interrogations. "How did that feel today? What was hard?"
  • Praise every visible improvement immediately and specifically.
How to close
Manager says:

"You have the right attitude and that matters more than you know. The skill piece we can fix — that is my job as much as yours. I am going to pair you with [trainer name] this week. I want you to ask questions, not just observe. Let's set a check-in for [date] and see where you are."

This is the most coachable quadrant. Attitude is harder to teach than skill. Invest here with urgency and confidence. A Gardener who moves from can't/will to can/will becomes one of your strongest culture carriers because they remember what it took to get there.
Can't + won't
No path forward — Coach out
Neither skill nor attitude. Every day you wait, you send a message to your top 20%.
＋
What's happening

This is the hardest quadrant to act on and the most costly to ignore. This Gardener is not performing and is not showing the attitude to improve. Every shift they work, your top performers absorb the slack. Every time you allow it without consequence, you tell your entire team that the standard is optional. That is a culture decision, not just a personnel one.

The accountability conversation
Manager says:

"I want to be honest with you because you deserve that. We have had [number] conversations about [specific behavior] and I have not seen the change I need to see. I am not here to surprise you — I want to give you one more clear picture of what needs to happen. [Specific standard] needs to look like [specific behavior] starting now. I am going to check in with you every shift. If I do not see consistent improvement by [specific date], we are going to need to make a change."

What must be true before this conversation
  • You have had at least two prior documented coaching conversations.
  • The standard was stated clearly and specifically — not implied.
  • You have given them a fair amount of time to improve.
  • You have ruled out a training gap — this is not a can't/will situation in disguise.
If there is no improvement
Manager says:

"We have talked about this several times and I have not seen the change I need. This is not the right fit right now. I appreciate the time you have put in here and I wish you well — but I need to make a change for the health of the team."

Parting with someone is an act of leadership, not a failure. Keeping someone in this quadrant is a failure — of your team, your top performers, and ultimately your guests. Do it with dignity. Do it clearly. Do it once you have done the work. But do it.
Before assigning a quadrant, ask three questions
1.
Do they know what the standard is? If not, this is a training problem, not a performance problem.
2.
Do they care about the standard? If not, understand the root cause before escalating.
3.
Do they have low personal standards? The most honest signal for the can't/won't quadrant.

This is a "can/won't" situation. The skill is there. Coach the behavior directly: "Every time you are late, it shifts the load to your teammates before you arrive. That's not the standard here." This is likely "can't/will." A training problem, not a performance problem. Before coaching, verify they actually know the standard. Assign a trainer from your top 20% to work alongside them. Give them a clear timeline for improvement and check in daily.
A strong team member is not getting recognized and seems disengaged.
This is a leadership failure, not a performance problem. Praise more than you criticize. If you are not recognizing your top performers publicly and consistently, you are telling them their effort is invisible. Correct this immediately. Praise is free and it is powerful.
A team member blames the kitchen every time a guest complaint arises.
Below the line behavior: finger pointing and cover your tail. This directly violates Self-Responsibility and Own the Experience. Coach the See It, Own It response directly. Nothing changes after multiple conversations with a low performer.
You have done the work. You have been constant, gentle, and applied pressure. You have documented it. Keeping this person is a message to your entire team that standards are negotiable. Parting with someone is one of the hardest acts of leadership. It is also one of the most important.

Attendance and reliability
A Gardener calls off frequently, often on weekends or high-volume shifts.
First, separate the pattern from the individual incident. A single callout is not a coaching moment. A pattern is. Your job is to see it clearly before you respond to it.
First callout
Acknowledge it without penalty. Life happens.
Note the date, shift, and reason in your log.
No formal conversation needed at this stage.
A pattern emerges
Have the conversation directly and privately.
Name the pattern without accusations: "I've noticed you've called off three of the last five Sundays."
Ask a genuine question before assuming intent.
What to say in the coaching conversation
"When you call off, your teammates carry your section. That creates a harder shift for them and a harder experience for our guests. I want to understand what's going on and figure out how we fix it together."
Three things the conversation needs to uncover
1.
Is something real going on? A schedule conflict, personal situation, health issue? If so, solve it with them, not around them.
2.
Do they understand the impact on teammates and guests? If not, make it concrete. This is an ownership conversation.
3.
Is this a can/won't situation? If they understand the impact and the pattern continues, this is now an accountability decision, not a scheduling conversation.
The standard is the standard on your best shift and your worst one
Your top 20% show up. They cover. They carry the team. Every time you let a callout pattern go unaddressed, you tax your best people and reward below the line behavior. That erodes culture faster than almost anything else.
