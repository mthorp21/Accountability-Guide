Above the line vs below the line
Ownership behaviors
Above the line — How to praise it
Take ownership. Coach with dignity. Center the salt shaker every time.
＋
What above the line looks like
  • Takes ownership of a problem immediately, without being asked.
  • Acknowledges a gap in the standard and addresses it on the spot.
  • Coaches a teammate with dignity — direct, clear, and without humiliating them.
  • Follows through on every commitment they make to a guest or a teammate.
  • Centers the salt shaker every time, even on a slammed Saturday.
Why praising it matters

Above the line behavior is easy to miss because it looks like the job being done normally. It is not. It is the job being done at the standard you are trying to build. If you only notice it when it is missing, you are managing failure instead of building culture. Praising above the line behavior in the moment tells your entire team what the bar looks like.

How to praise it — in the moment
Specific, public, right away:

"I just want to call out what [name] did when that situation came up — instead of waiting for someone else to handle it, they owned it and took care of the guest immediately. That is exactly what above the line looks like. That is the standard."

Direct, private, personal:

"I saw how you handled that and I want you to know I noticed. That is not a small thing. You did not wait, you did not look around for someone else to step in. You just took care of it. That is the kind of Gardener we need more of here."

Praise rules at Morning Belle
  • Be specific. "Good job today" means nothing. "The way you handled table 12 when the order came out wrong — that was above the line" means everything.
  • Be immediate. Praise at the end of a shift is better than nothing. Praise in the moment is what builds the behavior.
  • Be public when it benefits the team. Calling it out in pre-shift or during a team moment sets the visible standard for everyone, not just the person you are praising.
  • Never manufacture it. Gardeners can tell the difference between recognition that is earned and recognition that is routine. Only say it when you mean it.
The ratio matters. If you are correcting more than you are praising, you are either not looking for the right things, or you have a deeper team problem that needs addressing. Both are worth examining.
Avoidance behaviors
Below the line — How to address it
Wait and see. Finger pointing. Cover your tail. "It's not my job."
＋
What below the line looks like
  • Wait and see — "Someone else will handle it." The problem sits while the Gardener watches.
  • Cover your tail — "The kitchen messed it up." "Manager A told me to do it that way." Deflecting responsibility onto others.
  • Ignore or deny — "That table was fine. I checked on them." When the evidence says otherwise.
  • Finger pointing — "Well, so-and-so doesn't do it either." Using others' behavior to justify their own.
  • "It's not my job" — Refusing ownership because it falls outside a perceived lane.
How to address it — in the moment

Below the line behavior addressed immediately is a coaching moment. Below the line behavior ignored is an endorsed standard. You do not need to make it a big moment — you need to make it a clear one.

For wait and see / "it's not my job":

"I need you to own that in the moment. When you see a problem, your job is to take care of it — not to wait for someone else to catch it. I am not asking you to fix everything alone. I am asking you to take the first step every time."

For finger pointing / cover your tail:

"I hear you. And right now I want to focus on your role in this. What could you have done differently? Not what they did — what could you have done? That is where we are going to start."

For ignore or deny:

"I want to give you the benefit of the doubt, and I also want to be honest with you about what I observed. Here is what I saw: [specific situation]. I am not here to argue about it — I am here to make sure it does not happen again. What do you think got in the way?"

When it becomes a pattern

A single below the line moment is a coaching opportunity. Three of the same behavior is a pattern. A pattern requires a direct conversation with documentation, a clear expectation, and a specific follow-up date. At that point you are no longer in a teaching moment — you are in an accountability conversation.

  • Name the pattern explicitly. "This is the third time I have seen you respond this way when something goes wrong."
  • Connect it to impact. "When you point to someone else, the guest's problem does not get solved and your teammates absorb the work. That is not fair to either of them."
  • Ask for ownership. "What are you going to do differently starting today?"
  • Set a follow-up. "I am going to check in with you on this at the end of your next shift. Not to catch you — to support you."
Below the line behavior that goes unaddressed becomes your culture. Every Gardener who witnesses you walking past it learns that it is acceptable. You do not have to make it dramatic — you have to make it consistent.

Missing pressure
Constant + Gentle only
No growth. Team lacks drive and passion.

What it looks like

You correct every time and you do it kindly. But the salt shaker never moves back to center because there is no real consequence for missing the standard. The team reads this as the bar being optional. Over time, effort and performance drift downward and morale follows because your best Gardeners notice that everyone gets the same outcome regardless of how they perform.

Common signs you are here
  • You have the same conversation with the same person multiple times without change.
  • You find yourself softening the message so much that the urgency disappears.
  • Your top performers seem frustrated or disengaged.
  • You avoid follow-up because you don't want another uncomfortable conversation.
How to return to center
What to add back:

Name the consequence clearly and specifically. Not as a threat — as a reality. "I have brought this up before and I need you to understand that if this does not change, it affects your schedule, your standing on the team, and ultimately whether this is the right place for you."

  • After each coaching conversation, write down what was said and what will happen if nothing changes. Say both parts out loud.
  • Set a specific follow-up date. Check in on that date no matter what.
  • Ask yourself: if this behavior continued for 90 more days, would you still have this person on the team? If the answer is no, the pressure needs to be in your next conversation, not your 10th.
  • Practice the closing line before the conversation. Saying it out loud once makes it easier to say in the moment.
Missing constant
Gentle + Pressure only
Mixed messages. Rules feel optional.
＋
What it looks like

You coach well when you catch something, and you hold the standard when you do — but you don't catch it every time. You let it go on a busy Saturday. You see it and decide to address it later, and later never comes. The team learns that the standard only applies when you are watching, in the right mood, or have the time. That is not a standard. That is a preference.

Common signs you are here
  • You correct the same behavior on Monday but not on Friday.
  • You tell yourself "I'll catch it next time" and then don't.
  • The team performs differently depending on who is managing the shift.
  • You feel like some team members only comply when you are present.
How to return to center
What to add back:

Build a physical habit around correction. Walk the floor with intention. Create a pre-shift mental checklist of the top two or three standards you will watch for that day. When you see a miss, address it in the moment — not at the end of the shift, not in the next 1-on-1. Now.

  • Identify the one or two behaviors you most often let slide. Name them. Put them on your radar before every shift.
  • After each shift, ask yourself: did I miss anything I should have addressed? Write it down and bring it up at the next interaction — not to reopen a wound, but to close the loop.
  • Use pre-shifts to reset the standard out loud. When the whole team hears the expectation at the start of the shift, correction mid-shift feels less personal and more expected.
  • Hold the standard equally across all team members. Inconsistency often shows up as favoritism, even when it is not intended.
Missing gentle
Constant + Pressure only
Burnout. Loss of graciousness.
＋
What it looks like

You correct every time and you hold the line every time. But the delivery strips people of their dignity. Correction without care reads as criticism without investment. The team may comply in the short term, but trust erodes. Gardeners stop asking questions, stop taking initiative, and start looking for an exit. The standard is being enforced but the culture is being destroyed in the process.

Common signs you are here
  • Team members go quiet when you walk into a room.
  • Gardeners are technically compliant but show no energy or ownership.
  • You are correcting the same people repeatedly but the relationship is getting worse, not better.
  • Turnover is higher than it should be for a team that knows the job.
How to return to center
What to add back:

Before every correction, ask yourself one question: am I coaching this person or am I just correcting them? Coaching requires you to be on their side. Lead with what you observed, not what they did wrong. "I want to help you nail this" lands differently than "you did that wrong." Same standard. Completely different relationship.

  • For every correction you give in a shift, find at least one thing to genuinely praise. Not manufactured — real. Train yourself to look for it.
  • Check your timing. Correcting someone in front of guests or peers removes their dignity. Pull them aside. Same message, delivered with respect.
  • After a coaching moment, follow up with that person later in the shift — not about the correction, just a normal check-in. It signals that the correction was about the behavior, not about them as a person.
  • Ask yourself honestly: do the people I lead know that I am in their corner? If you are not sure, that is your answer.

When

Why
The team member must understand why the standard exists, not just that they missed it.

Who this is — and why it matters to get it right

The middle 60% is the most important group to understand correctly, because it is not one type of person. It splits into two distinct quadrants and they need completely different coaching approaches. Getting them confused is one of the most common management mistakes.

Type 1: Can't + Will — skill gap, right attitude

This Gardener wants to be here and tries hard. The inconsistency is about capability, not commitment. They do not yet have the skill to perform at the top 20% level, but the attitude is there to build on.

  • Pair with a top 20% trainer and give them specific, time-bound skill targets. "By end of next week, I want to see you suggestive-selling on every table without prompting."
  • Check in daily. Short, specific, encouraging. "How did that go today? What felt hard? Here is what I noticed."
  • Praise every visible step forward immediately. Skill-building requires confidence. Your recognition accelerates it.
  • This Gardener is on a path to the top 20% if you invest correctly. Do not let them drift because you are busy with harder problems.
Type 2: Can + Won't — skill present, attitude inconsistent

This Gardener can do the job. You have seen it. But they choose not to consistently. This is an attitude or motivation issue. It requires a direct conversation about why, not a training plan.

  • Do not assign a trainer. They do not need training. Sending them back through onboarding sends the wrong message and wastes a trainer's time.
  • Have the direct conversation. "I have seen you do this right. I need to understand what is getting in the way." Then listen before you coach.
  • Name the impact. "When you are inconsistent, it creates extra work for your teammates and an uneven experience for guests. That is not fair to either of them."
  • Watch the pattern. A Can + Won't Gardener who does not move with constant gentle pressure becomes your next bottom 20% problem. Do not wait.
The key question before every middle-60% coaching conversation: Is this a skill problem or an attitude problem? The answer determines everything about how you respond. Ask it every time.

Who this is

This is your Can't + Won't quadrant. Often, this is a Can + Won't Gardener that was never coached directly enough, long enough. The attitude did not improve because the pressure was never real. Now the skill has atrophied or was never there to begin with. Either way, the result is the same: below standard performance with no meaningful movement toward improvement.

What keeping them costs
  • Your top 20% absorbs their slack every shift. Extra tables, covered sections, picked-up side work. It adds up and they notice.
  • Your middle 60% uses them as a benchmark. If the bottom 20% can stay, "good enough" becomes the visible standard.
  • Your guests feel it. Inconsistent service is not invisible. It shows up in reviews, repeat visit rates, and server tip averages.
  • Your own credibility as a leader is at stake. Your team watches what you allow. What you allow is what you are endorsing.
The right sequence before acting
  • Confirm this is not a Can't + Will situation in disguise. Have you actually provided the training and tools? If not, that comes first.
  • Confirm you have had at least two documented conversations with specific standards and specific follow-up dates.
  • Confirm the expectation was stated clearly — not implied, not hinted at, not buried in a general pre-shift note.
  • If all three are true and nothing has changed, you have done your part. The next conversation is the accountability conversation.
